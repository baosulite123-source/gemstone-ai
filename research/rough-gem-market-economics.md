## 3. Current Rough Gem Grading & Sorting

### The Manual Grading Process

Rough gemstone grading remains **overwhelmingly manual** and relies on expert human judgment. There is no universally standardized grading system for rough colored stones comparable to the GIA diamond grading system.

#### Key Parameters Assessed
1. **Color** â€” Hue, saturation, tone (assessed visually under standardized lighting)
2. **Clarity** â€” Inclusions visible to naked eye or loupe; internal fractures, silk, crystals
3. **Shape/Crystal Form** â€” Affects cutting yield potential
4. **Size (Weight)** â€” Measured in carats
5. **Transparency** â€” Transparent, translucent, opaque
6. **Treatment Potential** â€” Whether heat treatment could improve color/clarity
7. **Origin Indicators** â€” Crystal habit, inclusions suggesting geographic origin

#### How Sorting Works in Practice

**At the mine level:**
- Rough is washed, sorted into broad categories (gem-grade, near-gem, industrial).
- Initial sorting by size using sieves (calibrated mesh screens).
- Color sorting done by eye under daylight or standardized lighting.
- Speed: An experienced sorter can process **200â€“500 stones per hour** for basic categorization.

**At trading hubs (Bangkok, Colombo):**
- Expert dealers ("sorters" or "assorters") examine individual stones.
- Each stone is assessed for color, clarity, shape, and cutting potential.
- Stones are grouped into "lots" of similar quality for sale.
- Speed: Detailed individual assessment takes **2â€“5 minutes per stone** for an expert.
- A skilled sorter might evaluate **100â€“200 stones per day** at full detail.

**Formal auction preparation (e.g., Gemfields):**
- Rough is sorted into lots by gemologists over weeks/months.
- Lots are categorized into defined quality tiers (e.g., Gemfields uses ~60+ quality grades for rough ruby).
- Photography, weighing, and documentation for each lot.
- The process from mine to auction-ready can take **3â€“6 months**.

### Limitations of Manual Grading

- **Subjectivity:** Two experts can disagree on color grade or quality tier, especially for borderline stones. Studies suggest **15â€“30% disagreement rates** on color grading between experienced gemologists.
- **Fatigue:** Accuracy degrades after 4â€“6 hours of continuous sorting.
- **Inconsistency:** Grading can vary by time of day, lighting conditions, and sorter fatigue.
- **Bottleneck:** The scarcity of truly expert sorters limits throughput.
- **Bias:** Sorters may unconsciously bias toward expected origin-quality correlations.
- **No data capture:** Manual sorting generates minimal structured data for analytics.

## 5. The Economic Case for AI Automation in Rough Gem Grading

### 5.1 Cost Savings from Faster/More Accurate Grading

- **5â€“25x throughput improvement**, reducing labor costs per stone by 80â€“95%

**Direct labor savings:**
- A medium-scale operation sorting 10,000 stones/month currently requires 3â€“5 expert sorters

- Annual labor savings: **$100,000â€“300,000** per operation

**Accuracy improvement:**
- Eliminating subjective disagreement (15â€“30% of borderline decisions) reduces pricing errors

- Machine learning models trained on thousands of expert-graded examples can match or exceed average human accuracy for color grading

### 5.2 Reduced Losses from Misgrading

**The misgrading problem:**
- Misgrading a rough stone by one quality tier can mean a **20â€“50% pricing error**
- On a parcel of 1,000 stones averaging $50/ct (10ct average), a 5% misgrading rate affecting 50 stones with an average $25/stone error = **$1,250 loss per parcel**
- Scaled across annual volumes of millions of carats: **$500,000â€“5,000,000** in annual value leakage from misgrading

**Specific misgrading risks:**
- **Color under-grading:** Selling a fine stone in a commercial lot (loss: $100â€“500+ per stone)
- **Color over-grading:** Overpaying at auction for stones that don't meet expectations (loss: 20â€“40% of purchase price)
- **Missing treatment potential:** Not identifying stones that would dramatically improve with heat treatment (loss: 2â€“5x value)
- **Incorrect origin attribution:** Missing indicators that suggest premium origin (loss: 2â€“10x value premium)

**AI mitigation:**
- Consistent, data-driven classification reduces misgrading rate from ~15â€“30% to potentially **3â€“5%**

### 5.3 Improved Cutting Yield Value

**The cutting optimization opportunity:**

- Rough-to-cut weight retention: typically **30â€“50%**
- But **value retention** is what matters â€” a well-oriented cut can yield a stone worth 3â€“5x more than a poorly oriented cut of the same rough
- Key factors AI can optimize:
  - **Color zoning analysis:** Identifying optimal orientation to maximize face-up color
  - **Inclusion mapping:** 3D scanning to plan cuts that avoid or minimize visible inclusions
  - **Shape optimization:** Recommending optimal cut shape (oval, cushion, round, etc.) for maximum value
  - **Weight retention vs. quality trade-off:** Calculating whether a larger, slightly included stone is worth more than a smaller, cleaner one

**Value impact:**
- Studies in diamond cutting (where AI is more advanced) show **10â€“15% improvement** in yield value through optimized planning
- For colored stones, the potential is even larger due to greater color/inclusion complexity
- On a $50M annual cutting operation, a 10% yield improvement = **$5M additional value**
- Even a conservative 5% improvement on a small operation ($5M/year) = **$250,000** additional value

### 5.4 Scalability Advantages

**Human expert scarcity:**
- Truly skilled rough gem sorters require 5â€“10 years of training
- The global supply of expert rough ruby/sapphire sorters is estimated at only **a few hundred** individuals
- New deposit discoveries (like Montepuez in 2009) create sudden demand spikes for sorting expertise

**Data network effects:**

- Cross-deposit learning: Patterns from one mine can inform grading at another
- Market intelligence: Aggregate grading data enables better pricing and demand forecasting
